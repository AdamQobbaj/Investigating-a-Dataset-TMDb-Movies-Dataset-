This data set contains information about 10,000 movies collected from The Movie Database (TMDb), including user ratings and revenue.

Column Description¶

  • id: Unique identifier for the movie.
  • imdb_id: IMDb identifier for the movie.
  • popularity: Popularity score of the movie.
  • budget: Budget of the movie in dollars.
  • revenue: Revenue generated by the movie in dollars.
  • original_title: Original title of the movie.
  • cast: List of main actors in the movie.
  • homepage: Official website of the movie.
  • director: Name of the movie's director.
  • tagline: The tagline or slogan of the movie.
  • keywords: Keywords associated with the movie.
  • overview: Brief description or summary of the movie.
  • runtime: Duration of the movie in minutes.
  • genres: Genres of the movie (e.g., Action, Comedy).
  • production_companies: Companies involved in producing the movie.
  • release_date: Release date of the movie.
  • vote_count: Number of votes received on TMDb.
  • vote_average: Average rating of the movie.
  • release_year: Year the movie was released.
  • budget_adj: Inflation-adjusted budget.
  • revenue_adj: Inflation-adjusted revenue.

# Groups the dataframe by `group_col` and computes the average for columns in `value_cols`.
# Returns the resulting dataframe.
def group_and_average(df, group_col, value_cols):
    grouped_df = df.groupby(group_col)[value_cols].mean().reset_index()
    grouped_df.rename(columns={col: f'avg_{col}' for col in value_cols}, inplace=True)
    return grouped_df

# Plots a pie chart given a dataframe, value column, label column, and chart title.
def plot_pie_chart(df, value_col, label_col, title):
    plt.figure(figsize=(8,8))
    plt.pie(df[value_col], labels=df[label_col], autopct='%1.1f%%', startangle=140)
    plt.title(title)
    plt.axis('equal')
    plt.show()

Limitations¶

While this analysis provided valuable insights, several limitations should be considered:

  • Missing or incomplete data: Some movies may have missing or zero values for important fields like budget or revenue, which could skew the averages and visualizations.
  • Outliers: High-budget blockbusters or exceptionally low-budget films can impact mean values. A median or trimmed mean might provide a different perspective.
  • Temporal component ignored: Since this analysis did not break down trends over time (e.g., yearly), it does not reveal how genre-specific budgets or revenues may have evolved historically.
  • Data bias: The dataset may primarily feature more well-known or commercially successful films, possibly underrepresenting independent or niche movies.

Conclusions¶

Throughout this project, we performed an exploratory data analysis on a movie dataset with a focus on understanding how various features such as genre, popularity, budget, and revenue relate to one another. The primary objective was to derive meaningful insights from the data and present them through effective visualizations and statistical summaries.

We began by cleaning and preparing the dataset to ensure it was ready for analysis. This included addressing missing values, filtering out anomalies, and selecting relevant columns for our investigation. Once the dataset was refined, we grouped the data by genre, which allowed us to compute the average values of key metrics like popularity, budget, and revenue for each genre.

From this grouping, some interesting trends emerged:

Certain genres, such as Adventure, consistently showed higher average budgets, likely reflecting the industry's tendency to invest heavily in blockbuster films. In contrast, other genres operated on smaller budgets but still achieved significant popularity or profitability. The pie chart visualization of average budgets per genre provided a clear and intuitive representation of how production spending is distributed across different genres. Additionally, by identifying the top five genres by average budget, we gained a more focused view of which genres dominate industry spending. These findings could be crucial for stakeholders such as producers, investors, or market analysts who seek to understand spending patterns in filmmaking.

Further Exploration¶

While this analysis provided valuable insights, it also opened up new questions worth exploring:

How does the revenue-to-budget ratio vary by genre, and which genres tend to be more profitable? Does popularity correlate strongly with revenue or budget within each genre? How have these trends evolved over time, and do certain genres perform better during specific periods (e.g., summer blockbusters)? Could other factors such as director reputation, cast size, or marketing expenses further explain these patterns?

Final Thoughts¶
